Why These Three Metrics

Every decision, hire, and investment in this business should ladder back to one of these three. They were chosen because together they answer the questions that revenue alone cannot.

01 Contribution Profit Are we getting richer, or just bigger?
In plain English
Revenue, minus what it cost to make the product, minus what we spent on ads to sell it.

This is the money left over after we've made the product and paid to acquire the customer. Everything else — team, ops, software, salaries — comes out of this pool. We track this instead of revenue because you can grow revenue while destroying value. If we spent $80 on ads to bring in a $100 sale, and it cost $25 to make the product, we lost $5 on that transaction. Revenue looked fine. Contribution profit caught the problem immediately.

Our product margin is approximately 4x — meaning for every dollar it costs to make, we sell it for four. That gives us a ~75% gross margin before ads, which is exceptional for a physical product. The job of this metric is to protect that ratio as we scale.

How it works — one $100 sale
Customer pays$100.00
Cost to make + ship (~25% COGS based on 4x product margin)−$25.00
Ad spend to acquire that customer (at 4x ROAS — $25 in, $100 back)−$25.00
Contribution profit per sale$50.00 (50%)

Why not just use revenue? Because revenue is a vanity metric on its own. The same revenue number can mean very different things depending on how much was spent to generate it. Two weeks ago we had $397K gross sales. Last week $222K. Revenue looks like it halved. But if ad spend also came down and margin held, contribution profit may have been similar. Revenue alone doesn't tell you that story.

Why not just use profit? Full profit requires knowing every cost — team, warehousing, software, ops. Those are hard to track week to week. Contribution profit is the version we can calculate in real time from data we already have. It's not the final answer, but it's the most honest leading indicator we can track consistently.

The 3x floor gives us buffer for costs not yet in this model (team, ops, 3PL). At 3x ROAS on a 75% gross margin product, the business is still generating meaningful contribution profit even after untracked costs. Below 3x, we are likely running at a loss on those customers once everything is counted. This threshold applies to every individual campaign — not just the blended average.

Returns — the silent drain on contribution profit

Every return erodes contribution profit directly — we've already spent the COGS and the ad spend, and now we're giving the revenue back. Our all-time return rate is 1.1% of gross sales, which is very healthy. However, $146K of all-time returns (75% of the total) are unattributed to any product in our data — this needs investigation before we can understand where returns are actually coming from.

Target: grow monthly contribution profit from ~$668K toward $900K within 12 months. Lever 1: fix underperforming Meta campaigns (4 of 9 currently below the 3x floor). Lever 2: grow subscriptions to reduce acquisition cost per repeat customer.
02 Habit Loop Retention Do customers come back on their own?
In plain English
The % of first-time customers who buy again within 90 days — without being paid to through heavy discounts.

This is the most honest signal of whether the product actually works. If customers come back on their own, the product is delivering on its promise. If they don't, no amount of ad spend fixes that long term — the business is just running a leaky bucket, spending money to fill it while it drains just as fast.

Repeat customers are also dramatically more profitable than new ones. A repeat customer costs almost nothing to re-acquire, so nearly the full product margin flows through as contribution profit. Growing the proportion of revenue from returning customers is one of the highest-leverage things we can do for profitability without touching a single ad campaign.

Why subscriptions are the strongest version of this signal: A subscription means a customer has decided, without being reminded, to keep the product in their life permanently. They've built it into their routine. That's the habit loop confirmed. Our subscription programme has generated $971K in lifetime revenue since April 2025, and 67.2% of that is recurring — meaning most subscription revenue comes from people who signed up and kept going, not just tried it once.

The biggest untapped opportunity in this metric: Only 3.1% of checkout orders include a subscription product. The product is clearly sticky once people subscribe — 67% recurring revenue is proof. But the vast majority of customers are buying once without subscribing. Converting more of them at checkout doesn't require more ad spend — it requires better subscription merchandising on the product page and at checkout. Every 100 new subscribers adds ~$6,200/month in MRR permanently.

What we still need: The 90-day repeat purchase rate for customers who buy without subscribing. This is the missing piece — it would tell us whether the habit loop is working for the majority of customers who haven't formally subscribed. This data needs to be pulled from Shopify.

Target: rebuild subscriptions from 910 to 1,400. Grow checkout subscription conversion from 3.1% to 6%+. Pull 90-day repeat purchase rate from Shopify to complete the picture.
03 Revenue Resilience What survives if Meta goes down tomorrow?
In plain English
The % of revenue that keeps flowing without any paid ad spend — our floor if everything else stops.

Every business that relies heavily on one paid channel is one policy change, one algorithm update, or one bad month of creative away from a revenue crisis. This metric exists to make sure we are actively building against that risk — not just hoping it doesn't happen.

The floor is calculated as: subscription MRR plus email flow revenue, divided by total weekly revenue. These are the two revenue streams that keep running without anyone actively spending on campaigns. Right now that floor is approximately 14.8% of weekly revenue — meaning if all paid ads stopped tomorrow, we'd retain roughly 15 cents of every dollar we currently make. The target is 35%, which would mean the business could survive a full month of zero paid activity without existential risk.

Why this matters right now: Meta CPA has risen for three consecutive weeks ($34.63 → $40.30 → $49.77). That trend is a live demonstration of channel dependency — one channel deteriorating and dragging the whole business's efficiency with it. The answer is not just to fix the campaigns (though we should). It's to build a floor high enough that we're never fully at the mercy of any single channel.

The fastest way to build the floor is subscriptions. Subscription MRR flows every month with zero acquisition cost. Email flows are the second pillar — healthy now but partially dependent on traffic, so they don't fully count as independent. Every new subscriber is a permanent addition to the floor.
